\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Contributions}

This thesis has investigated the application of genomic foundation models to metagenomic taxonomic classification through a token-level representation approach. Our main findings are:

\textbf{Token-level representations improve classification.} By preserving the full sequence of token embeddings from the Nucleotide Transformer v2 backbone---rather than discarding positional information through mean pooling---and aggregating them via a learnable attention-pooling head, we achieve genus-level accuracy improvements over frozen-embedding baselines. The token-level approach with LoRA fine-tuning achieves 63.05\% RC TTA accuracy on 120 genera with 5M balanced training reads, scaling to 66.06\% with 50M reads, compared to 51.70\% for the frozen mean-pool baseline.

\textbf{Data volume is the dominant performance factor within a fixed architecture.} Through systematic data scaling experiments spanning 500K to 50M training reads, we demonstrate that increasing training data volume by $10\times$ yields $+7.76$ percentage points in accuracy and $+12.27$ percentage points in macro F1, while extensive architectural and training technique ablations yield at most $\pm0.5$ percentage points. This finding aligns with the observations of Wichmann et al.~\cite{Wichmann2023}, who achieve 98.3\% genus recall (98.9\% precision) using a large-scale balanced training dataset.

\textbf{Tokenization is the dominant cross-architecture design factor.} Controlled ablations training MetaTransformer~\cite{Wichmann2023} on the same 50M balanced dataset reveal that overlapping 12-mer tokenization provides a \textbf{+31.83~pp} advantage over non-overlapping 6-mer tokenization (78.83\% vs.\ 47.00\%), even before convergence. This effect nearly doubles the pre-training advantage measured under fixed tokenization (+18.29~pp for NT-v2 vs.\ MetaTransformer-6mer). The primary limitation of our GFM-based approach is therefore NT-v2's 6-mer tokenization constraint, not its backbone capacity or adaptation strategy.

\textbf{Class balance is critical for rare taxa.} Balanced subsampling across species reduces the number of F1$=$0 classes from 9 to 2 and improves macro F1 by $+12.27$ percentage points, demonstrating that class imbalance---not model capacity---is the primary cause of poor performance on rare taxa.

\textbf{RC TTA is a robust, free improvement.} Reverse-complement test-time augmentation consistently improves accuracy by $+1$ to $+1.5$ percentage points across all experimental conditions, requiring no additional training cost.

\textbf{Current hardware is sufficient.} The NVIDIA H100 80~GB GPU is adequately provisioned for this workload, with VRAM utilization below 50\% even at batch size 256. The operational bottleneck is the SLURM time limit rather than computational resources.

\section{Limitations}

This work has several limitations that should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Simulated data only}: All experiments use reads simulated by ART from reference genomes. Real metagenomic reads contain additional noise (host contamination, chimeric reads, strain-level variation) that may degrade classifier performance.
    
    \item \textbf{Limited taxonomic scope}: The reference database covers 120 genera and 1,535 species of human gut microorganisms. Performance on environmental samples with broader taxonomic diversity is unknown.
    
    \item \textbf{Single read length}: All experiments use 150~bp reads. Performance at other read lengths (e.g., 250~bp for Illumina MiSeq, or long reads from Oxford Nanopore / PacBio) has not been evaluated.
    
    \item \textbf{Incomplete data scaling}: Training on the full 258M dataset was not completed at the time of writing, leaving the upper bound of the scaling trajectory unconfirmed. The v9 (50M) experiment is complete, achieving 66.06\% RC TTA accuracy.
    
    \item \textbf{No streaming data loader}: Current implementation loads all training reads into memory, limiting scalability beyond $\sim$50M reads without substantial RAM ($>$50~GB).
    
    \item \textbf{Comparison scope}: Direct comparison with MetaTransformer is confounded by differences in training data volume, making it difficult to isolate the contribution of architectural choices.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Future Work}

Several promising directions emerge from this work:

\subsection{Backbone Ablation and Architectural Insights}

The shallow Transformer ablation (Section~\ref{sec:exp-shallow}) directly addresses whether the 29-layer pre-trained backbone justifies its computational cost over a simpler architecture. The results of this experiment will inform the architectural direction of the system:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If the pre-trained backbone significantly outperforms the shallow variant, future work should explore \emph{deeper} or \emph{domain-specifically pre-trained} backbones, such as models trained on the GTDB database~\cite{Parks2022} ($>$400,000 microbial genomes).
    \item If the shallow variant approaches the pre-trained backbone's performance, the practical recommendation shifts toward lightweight, task-specific models with better tokenization (e.g., 12-mers), trading pre-training for efficiency.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Full-Scale Data Training}

The most immediate next step is completing training on the full 258M-read dataset. Our data scaling analysis predicts that this could yield genus-level accuracy in the 82--90\% range, substantially narrowing the gap with MetaTransformer. Achieving this requires either:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Implementing a streaming data loader that reads from disk, eliminating the in-memory requirement.
    \item Requesting extended SLURM time limits (72+ hours) or developing a multi-job training orchestration system.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Species-Level Scaling and Hierarchical Classification}

Species classification at the 500K scale achieved only $\sim$8\% accuracy (1,535 classes), with oracle-genus evaluation capping at $\sim$21\%. Training with 50M balanced reads (sp\_v4, Section~\ref{sec:exp-species-scaled}) is expected to dramatically improve species-level performance. Upon completion, the two-stage hierarchical classification pipeline can be evaluated:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Genus prediction (using the v9 model) to generate a candidate species set.
    \item Species classification within the candidate set (using the sp\_v4 model).
\end{enumerate}
The effectiveness of this approach scales directly with genus-level accuracy: at 66.06\% RC TTA accuracy (v9), top-5 routing covers the correct genus in $\sim$89.6\% of reads (v9 Top-5 accuracy), providing a substantially reduced candidate set for species discrimination.

\subsection{Tokenization Study}

The NT-v2 foundation model constrains tokenization to 6-mers. MetaTransformer's use of 12-mers suggests that longer tokens may be advantageous for taxonomic classification, as they directly capture longer characteristic motifs. A systematic comparison of tokenization strategies---including 6-mer, 12-mer, Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), and character-level encoding---under controlled backbone architectures would clarify whether tokenization or encoder depth is the more impactful design choice.

\subsection{Architectural Improvements}

\textbf{Multi-layer feature fusion}: Current experiments use only the last hidden layer of NT-v2. Combining representations from multiple layers (e.g., via weighted averaging or learned gating) could capture both low-level sequence features and high-level semantic information~\cite{DallaTorre2023}.

\textbf{Adapter fusion}: Training separate LoRA adapters for genus and species tasks and combining them through adapter fusion~\cite{Pfeiffer2021} could enable joint hierarchical classification without duplicating the backbone.

\subsection{Real-World Validation}

Validating the classifier on real metagenomic sequencing data from public repositories (e.g., Human Microbiome Project~\cite{HMP2012}) is essential for assessing practical applicability. This includes evaluating robustness to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Sequencing errors and quality variations across platforms.
    \item Host DNA contamination.
    \item Novel or uncharacterized taxa absent from the training database (out-of-distribution detection).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Efficiency Optimization}

For practical deployment in clinical or environmental monitoring settings, inference speed is critical. Potential optimizations include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model distillation}: Distilling the 498M-parameter LoRA-adapted model into a smaller task-specific model (e.g., 50M parameters).
    \item \textbf{Quantization}: Applying INT8 or INT4 quantization to reduce memory and accelerate inference.
    \item \textbf{Batched inference}: Optimizing the inference pipeline for high-throughput batch processing of millions of reads.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pre-training Strategy}

The current work fine-tunes a model pre-trained on 850 genomes from 174 species. A foundation model pre-trained specifically on a larger, more diverse collection of microbial genomes (e.g., the full GTDB database with $>$400,000 genomes~\cite{Parks2022}) may provide more suitable representations for metagenomic classification, reducing the amount of task-specific fine-tuning data required.

\section{Concluding Remarks}

This thesis demonstrates both the promise and the current limitations of applying genomic foundation models to metagenomic taxonomic classification. While token-level representations with LoRA fine-tuning provide a principled and effective framework, the results clearly indicate that \emph{sufficient training data is the prerequisite for competitive performance}. The gap between our current results and MetaTransformer's reported accuracy is attributable primarily to training data volume rather than architectural sophistication.

The backbone ablation study (comparing the 29-layer pre-trained NT-v2 with a single-layer Transformer trained from scratch) will provide direct evidence for whether the computational investment in large pre-trained backbones is justified for metagenomic classification, or whether lightweight task-specific models---paired with appropriate tokenization and sufficient training data---represent a more practical path forward. Together with the species-level scaling experiments and hierarchical classification pipeline, these ongoing investigations aim to establish a complete, competitive framework for GFM-based metagenomic taxonomic classification.
